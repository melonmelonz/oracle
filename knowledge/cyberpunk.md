# Cyberpunk

## The phrase that defines it

Cyberpunk is a science fiction subgenre and an aesthetic built on a single tension, usually written "high tech, low life." The future it imagines is wired, networked, and crowded with machines, but the people living in it are poor, hustling, surveilled, or modified against their will. The technology works; the society does not. Advanced computing, artificial intelligence, and body modification sit next to slums, addiction, and crime. The genre rarely offers a clean utopia or a clean apocalypse. Instead it gives a grimy, plausible middle: corporations that outlast governments, streets lit by advertising, and individuals who survive by knowing the system's seams. This pairing of dazzling capability with social rot is the load-bearing idea, and most cyberpunk works can be read as variations on it.

## Coining the word

The term comes from Bruce Bethke, a Minnesota writer who used it as the title of a short story. Accounts agree he wrote the story in spring 1980; it was published in Amazing Stories in 1983. Bethke has said he built the word deliberately, taking roots like "cyber" and "techno" and mixing them with terms for socially misdirected youth until one combination "just plain sounded right." The story itself, about teenage hackers, is not the genre's defining text; the word outgrew it almost immediately. Bethke later remarked that he had coined a label without inventing the thing it would come to name. That gap, between the coinage and the movement, is why credit for cyberpunk is usually split between the person who named it and the people who built it.

## Dozois and the naming of a movement

Gardner Dozois, then a prominent editor and later editor of Isaac Asimov's Science Fiction Magazine, is generally credited with popularizing "cyberpunk" as the name for a literary movement. In the early-to-mid 1980s he applied the word in print to a cluster of writers producing what he called bizarre, hard-edged, high-tech fiction, naming William Gibson and Bruce Sterling among them. The label stuck because it described a real shift. A loose group of writers was reacting against both the optimistic space opera of older science fiction and the soft introspection of the New Wave. They wanted computers, networks, and street-level grit. Dozois did not invent that impulse, but by attaching Bethke's word to it he gave critics and readers a handle, and the handle became the genre's name.

## Neuromancer and the Sprawl

William Gibson's first novel, Neuromancer (1984), became the genre's archetypal text and remains the work most people reach for first. It is, as of this writing, often cited as the only novel to win the Hugo, Nebula, and Philip K. Dick awards in a single sweep. The book follows Case, a burned-out hacker, through a world of artificial intelligences, corporate intrigue, and a navigable data-space Gibson called cyberspace; the word entered general use largely through him. Its setting, the Sprawl, is a continuous urban band along the American East Coast, formally the Boston-Atlanta Metropolitan Axis. Neuromancer opened the Sprawl trilogy, continued in Count Zero (1986) and Mona Lisa Overdrive (1988). Gibson's prose, dense with brand names and slang, did as much to define the genre's texture as its plot did.

## Mirrorshades and the canon

If Neuromancer was the genre's novel, Mirrorshades: The Cyberpunk Anthology (1986), edited by Bruce Sterling, was its manifesto in story form. Sterling, sometimes called cyberpunk's chief ideologue, had already been agitating for the movement through his fanzine Cheap Truth. Mirrorshades gathered twelve stories by the core writers: Gibson, Sterling, Pat Cadigan, Rudy Rucker, John Shirley, Lewis Shiner, Greg Bear, Tom Maddox, Marc Laidlaw, James Patrick Kelly, Paul Di Filippo, and collaborative pieces. The anthology is widely described as the first and still definitive collection of cyberpunk short fiction. The title itself became shorthand for the genre's look: the mirrored sunglasses worn by characters who watch without being seen. The collection both defined a canon and, by defining it, helped the movement begin to ossify and splinter.

## The visual genre: Blade Runner

Cyberpunk's most influential single object is arguably not a book but a film. Ridley Scott's Blade Runner (1982), loosely adapted from Philip K. Dick's 1968 novel Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?, gave the genre its visual vocabulary before Neuromancer was even published. Its Los Angeles is a rain-soaked, neon-drenched, perpetually dark city where vast corporate ziggurats loom over crowded streets and flying advertisements. Scott drew on Edward Hopper's paintings and the French comics magazine Metal Hurlant to build a high-tech but decaying future. That image, advanced technology layered over urban decay, became the template for how cyberpunk would be drawn, filmed, and rendered in games for decades. The film influenced later offshoots too, including biopunk, which centers on biotechnology and genetic engineering.

## Into anime and games

By the late 1980s cyberpunk had moved decisively beyond Anglophone print, and Japanese animation became one of its strongest carriers. Katsuhiro Otomo's Akira (1988), adapted from his own 1982 manga, brought a sprawling, violent vision of Neo-Tokyo to a global audience and set a standard for animated detail. Mamoru Oshii's Ghost in the Shell (1995), based on Masamune Shirow's 1989 manga, pushed the genre toward philosophy, asking what selfhood means when minds run on networks and bodies are interchangeable hardware. Its imagery of cascading code and networked consciousness fed directly back into Western film, including The Matrix. From there cyberpunk spread into tabletop and video games, from the Cyberpunk 2020 RPG to Deus Ex and the 2020 game Cyberpunk 2077, completing the genre's journey from a single short story's title into a durable, cross-media aesthetic.
