# Neuromancer and Cyberpunk

## The book

Neuromancer is William Gibson's first novel, published in 1984. It swept the three
major science fiction awards in one year, the Hugo, the Nebula, and the Philip K.
Dick award, which had never happened before. It is the founding text of the
cyberpunk genre: low-life and high-tech, a world of hackers, corporate states, and
machine intelligences, told in dense, jittery, neon prose.

The opening line is famous on its own: "The sky above the port was the color of
television, tuned to a dead channel." It sets the whole tone in fourteen words.

## The words it gave us

Gibson did not invent the idea of computer networks, but he named the feeling of
them. He popularized the word "cyberspace" and described it as a "consensual
hallucination," a shared graphical space that operators jack into directly. He also
leaned hard on the word "matrix" for that space, years before it became a movie.
The vocabulary of a generation of technology came partly out of this one book.

## The joke underneath it

The detail people love most is that Gibson wrote Neuromancer, the book that defined
the digital future, on a manual typewriter, a 1927 Hermes portable. He has said he
barely understood computers when he wrote it, and that this ignorance was an
advantage: he was free to imagine what they should feel like rather than be limited
by what they actually did. "The street finds its own uses for things," one of his
lines goes, and it has become a small law of how technology actually spreads.

## The story and the cast

The plot follows Case, a burned-out console cowboy whose ability to jack into
cyberspace has been deliberately damaged as punishment. He is hired, and repaired,
for one last job. Alongside him is Molly Millions, a razor-fingered street samurai
with mirrored lenses set into her face. The job is steered by two artificial
intelligences, Wintermute and Neuromancer, two halves of a mind trying to become
whole and free itself from the limits placed on it. The setting, the Sprawl, a
continuous urban mass on the eastern seaboard, became the backdrop for two more
novels and a template for decades of science fiction since.
