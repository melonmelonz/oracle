# The Legend of Zelda - Majora's Mask

## A game built out of its predecessor
Majora's Mask shipped for the Nintendo 64 in 2000; Japan on April 27, North America on October 26, and Europe on November 17. It is the sixth main Zelda game and the second to use 3D, following Ocarina of Time in 1998. The project began as an idea for a remixed "Ura" edition of Ocarina of Time for the 64DD disk peripheral. Eiji Aonuma had handled that game's dungeons and had no appetite for simply rebuilding them. Shigeru Miyamoto answered with a dare: could the team make a whole new game in one year if they reused the existing engine and models? They did it in about fifteen months. A smaller team led by Aonuma and Yoshiaki Koizumi reused character models, the engine, and graphic resources from Ocarina of Time, which had taken roughly four years to make. The game requires the Expansion Pak because of its higher memory use. Reuse was not only a schedule trick. Familiar faces from Hyrule, recontextualized into a darker setting, gave the world an uncanny weight.

## The three-day cycle
The heart of the game is a clock. The Skull Kid has pulled down the moon, and it crashes into Termina at the end of three days. The cycle runs about 54 minutes in real time. When the moon falls, or when Link plays the Song of Time, the world snaps back to the dawn of the first day. Resetting costs you. Rupees, consumables, and most progress vanish on the jump, though learned songs, acquired masks, and certain key items persist. That tension is the design. Every non-player character runs a fixed schedule across the 72 hours, so the same three days replay with the same events, and Link learns to be in the right place at the right hour. The structure rewards memory and planning over raw clearing of rooms. It also makes the world feel populated and doomed at once; people keep their small routines while the sky falls.

## Songs that bend the clock
The ocarina is the control panel for time. The Song of Time resets the loop and, on the N64 version, saves the game. The Inverted Song of Time is the Song of Time played backwards; it slows the flow of time to one-third speed on the N64, or to half speed in the 3DS remake, which buys breathing room inside a dungeon. It affects only time-linked events, not enemy attacks or Link's own movement speed. The Song of Double Time pushes the clock forward instead; on N64 it jumps to the next 6am or 6pm, while the 3DS version lets you pick an hour. The Song of Soaring warps Link to any activated Owl Statue. Together these melodies turn the deadline from a punishment into a resource the player schedules around.

## The transformation masks
Masks were an optional sidequest in Ocarina of Time. Here they are the core verb. The game holds 24 masks in total, three of which transform Link into another race. The Deku Mask, first acquired after the Happy Mask Salesman plays the Song of Healing, lets Link skip across water, fly briefly from Deku Flowers, and shoot bubbles; he is weak to fire. The Goron Mask carries the spirit of the fallen hero Darmani; it lets Link roll at speed, move heavy objects, and walk on lava. The Zora Mask holds Mikau, guitarist of the Indigo-Go's; it grants fast swimming and underwater combat with throwable fins. Each form changes not just combat but how Link reads the world, since each race relates differently to water, fire, and the residents of Termina. The three race masks are mandatory and cannot be traded away.

## The Fierce Deity
The Fierce Deity's Mask is the rarest transformation and the strangest to earn. Link can only acquire it on the moon, after trading all twenty non-transformation masks to the Moon Children waiting there. The child wearing Majora's Mask, sitting alone by the tree, hands it over once Link has nothing left to give, framing it as a game of good guys against bad guys in which Link is cast as the bad guy. The form is a tall adult swordsman wielding a two-handed blade that fires beams when Z-targeting and deals several times the damage of the basic sword. Its power is fenced in: on the N64 it works only during boss battles. The 3DS remake also allows the form inside the two fishing holes. It is not required to finish the game, but defeating Majora as Fierce Deity Link is treated as canon in the Hyrule Historia timeline.

## Termina and its mood
Termina is a parallel world, not Hyrule, and it wears Hyrule's faces with a sadder expression. Clock Town sits at the center, ringed by four regions. South lies the poisoned Southern Swamp and Woodfall, with the Deku Palace. North rises Snowhead, frozen, home to the Gorons. West opens onto Great Bay, with the Zora Hall, a marine lab, and the Pirates' Fortress. East runs Ikana Canyon, haunted by the undead in the Ancient Castle of Ikana. Link awakens the Four Giants to hold back the moon and confront Majora behind the mask. The Bombers Notebook tracks the residents' schedules and sidequests, a quiet ledger of small lives. The tone is what people remember; grief, masks, a countdown, the feeling that you are saving a place that already knows it is ending.

## The speedrun scene
Majora's Mask is a long-studied speedgame, and its leaderboards fracture into many categories: Any%, All Masks, All Dungeons, All Fairy Rewards, Unrestricted, No Major Glitches, and Glitchless. The N64 Zelda titles tend to splinter this way because each new major glitch reshapes routing. The time and song mechanics are themselves exploited as tools. Song of Time Storage, for instance, holds the reset action open so its state can be repurposed. Runners also use clipping out of bounds and wrong loading, where the game is tricked into loading a far-off room, the final boss, or the credits. The most dramatic break is the debug-menu route in Any%. A Deku Palace setup uses 63 plays of the Song of Soaring to reach the developer menu, after which the runner pulls boss remains and the Fierce Deity's Mask straight from that menu and rushes the ending. The trick does not work on the Virtual Console release and crashes on real N64 hardware, so runs use other setups. That route broke the one-hour barrier with a 49:42, where the prior Any% record had stood at 1:15:52 by ennopp112. Exact frame counts and the status of individual tricks shift over time, so specifics are best read as the state of the art rather than fixed facts.
